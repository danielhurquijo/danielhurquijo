Daniel Urquijo

I am a doctoral student at the European University Institute. I am interested in the politics of status, specifically, how high-status groups use politics to sustain social hierarchies and how low-status groups adapt, resist, and accept these. I explore these questions quantitatively using historical data and administrative records from 20th century South Africa. [CV].

The Politics of Transitional Justice: Evidence from South Africa
with A. Garmendia, and P. Zachary

Show Abstract

Abstract: Transitional justice institutions are political tools intended to delegitimize prior political regimes and legitimize new ones. For this reason, beneficiaries of the outgoing government tend to view them as threatening and illegitimate. We analyze the universe of amnesty decisions from the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission and show that such fears may be misplaced. Contrary to whitesâ€™ perceptions, amnesty decisions were not biased against the apartheid government. Instead, they consistently and uniquely favored members of the African National Congress (ANC) while punishing their direct political opponents, the Inkatha Freedom Party (IFP). Groups without formal representation in post-apartheid politics, such as non-ANC anti-apartheid organizations, did not receive special treatment. Our findings suggest that instances of political bias within transitional justice may be concentrated among electorally relevant groups, rather than solely directed against the preceding regime.
